1) Purpose, scope, and principles

These guidelines provide default preoperative instructions on fasting/diet and medication management for adult elective surgery. They are designed for anesthesia-led preoperative evaluation and are intended to reduce aspiration risk, perioperative hypoglycemia/hyperglycemia, medication withdrawal, and preventable complications (e.g., euglycemic DKA with SGLT2 inhibitors).

Principle: “Definitely hold/continue” is rare. When thrombotic risk, adrenal suppression, transplant immunosuppression, mechanical valves, recent coronary stent/ACS, brittle diabetes, or severe heart failure are present, coordinate with the managing specialty.

These recommendations align with major society guidance on preoperative fasting, perioperative diabetes medication safety, and perioperative management of antithrombotic therapy. See References

2) Fasting & diet restrictions (including GLP‑1 considerations)

Standard fasting (adults, elective)

  • Clear liquids: allowed until 2 hours before anesthesia/sedation start time.
  • Light meal (toast, non-fat): minimum fasting 6 hours.
  • Fatty meal / meat / fried foods: minimum fasting 8 hours.

Clear liquids include water, pulp-free juice, clear tea/coffee (no milk/cream), electrolyte drinks.

Source: ASA preoperative fasting guidelines. [1]

When to apply stricter diet instructions

  • Known or suspected delayed gastric emptying (gastroparesis, obstruction).
  • Active nausea/vomiting or severe reflux not controlled.
  • High-risk GLP‑1 RA patients (see Section 6).
  • Emergency surgery: manage as “full stomach.”

Sources: ASA fasting guidance and multi-society GLP‑1 perioperative guidance. [1,2]

GLP‑1 day-before clear liquids?
Not routinely. Most patients on GLP‑1 receptor agonists can follow standard fasting. Consider a 24-hour liquid-only diet only for higher-risk patients (dose escalation, significant GI symptoms, suspected gastroparesis) and/or apply aspiration-risk mitigation (Section 6). [2]

3) Medication management overview

Categories used in this document

  • CONTINUE — usually continue through day of surgery (DOS).
  • HOLD — usually hold/stop preoperatively with a typical interval.
  • ADJUST — dose reduction or schedule change rather than stopping.

Operational approach

  1. Identify meds with withdrawal risk (beta blockers, clonidine, anticonvulsants, steroids).
  2. Identify meds with fasting/hypoglycemia risk (insulin secretagogues, insulin).
  3. Identify meds with aspiration risk (GLP‑1 RA high-risk features).
  4. Identify meds with bleeding/thrombosis tradeoffs (antithrombotics).
  5. Document plan + last dose time in preop note.

4) Medications generally CONTINUED

Default: take morning doses with a sip of water unless otherwise specified. For time-sensitive meds (Parkinson’s, anticonvulsants), plan perioperative administration (PACU/ward).

Cardiovascular

  • Beta blockers (avoid withdrawal and periop tachycardia). [6] CONTINUE
  • Calcium channel blockers. CONTINUE
  • Statins. [6] CONTINUE
  • Clonidine (avoid rebound HTN). CONTINUE
  • Nitrates (individualize if hypotension risk). CONTINUE

Respiratory

  • Inhalers (ICS/LABA/LAMA), albuterol, nebulizers. CONTINUE
  • Montelukast. CONTINUE

Neurologic / psychiatric

  • Anticonvulsants (avoid seizures). CONTINUE
  • Parkinson medications (time-sensitive; coordinate dosing). CONTINUE
  • SSRIs/SNRIs — usually continue; consider bleeding/interaction risks case-by-case. CONTINUE
  • Chronic benzodiazepines — usually continue (avoid withdrawal). CONTINUE

Endocrine & other

  • Levothyroxine. CONTINUE
  • Chronic corticosteroids — continue; evaluate need for stress-dose coverage if adrenal suppression risk. [7] CONTINUE
  • Chronic opioids — continue baseline regimen; plan periop analgesia. CONTINUE
  • Acetaminophen. CONTINUE
  • Transplant immunosuppressants — do not stop without transplant team. CONTINUE

5) Medications generally HELD / ADJUSTED

High-priority “hold”:
  • SGLT2 inhibitors — hold preop to reduce euglycemic DKA risk. [3,4]

Diabetes medications (summary)

  • SGLT2 inhibitors: stop ≥3 days preop (often 4 days for ertugliflozin). [3,4] HOLD
  • Sulfonylureas: hold on DOS (hypoglycemia risk while NPO). HOLD
  • Metformin: hold on DOS; consider earlier hold with renal dysfunction or major surgery. [5] HOLD
  • Insulin: adjust rather than stop (Section 7). ADJUST
  • GLP‑1 RAs: usually continue; apply risk-based mitigation (Section 6). [2] RISK-BASED

Antihypertensives prone to intraop hypotension

  • ACE inhibitors / ARBs: commonly hold on DOS (lower risk of refractory hypotension). Individualize in select HF patients. [6] HOLD DOS
  • Diuretics: commonly hold on DOS to reduce hypovolemia/electrolyte derangements; consider continuing if decompensated HF/volume overload. HOLD DOS

NSAIDs & supplements

  • NSAIDs: stop interval depends on agent and surgical bleeding risk (often 3–7 days). SURGEON-DEPENDENT
  • Herbal supplements: stop 1–2 weeks preop when feasible due to bleeding/sedation/drug interactions. [8] HOLD

Other common holds

  • Phentermine and stimulant weight-loss agents: consider stopping ≥4–7 days preop (hemodynamic concerns). HOLD
  • PDE-5 inhibitors (e.g., sildenafil): consider holding on DOS in patients at risk for hypotension or if nitrate use is anticipated. CASE-BY-CASE
Antithrombotics: Do not stop anticoagulants/antiplatelets without documenting the indication (e.g., AF, VTE, recent stent) and the procedure bleeding risk. Default stop times vary by drug, renal function, and neuraxial plans. See Section 8.

6) GLP‑1 receptor agonists (GLP‑1 RA): risk stratification & diet plan

GLP‑1 RAs can delay gastric emptying and may increase risk of residual gastric contents in select patients. Current multi-society guidance supports continuation for most patients undergoing elective procedures, with additional precautions for higher-risk patients. [2]

LOW-RISK GLP‑1 RA patients CONTINUE

  • Stable maintenance dose (not in dose-escalation phase)
  • No significant GI symptoms (no persistent nausea/vomiting, severe bloating, early satiety)
  • No known gastroparesis or major motility disorder

Plan: continue GLP‑1 RA; standard fasting (Section 2). [2]

HIGH-RISK GLP‑1 RA patients MITIGATE

  • Dose escalation phase or recent dose increase
  • Moderate–severe GI symptoms (nausea/vomiting, abdominal pain, significant bloating/early satiety)
  • Known/suspected gastroparesis, or other causes of delayed emptying
  • Prior regurgitation/aspiration concerns

Plan options (departmental):

  1. Liquid-only diet for 24 hours preop (department may specify clear liquids only for highest risk). [2]
  2. Consider aspiration precautions and/or anesthesia technique modifications.
  3. If severe symptoms or active escalation and case is elective, consider postponement.
  4. Optional: point-of-care gastric ultrasound if available and expertise exists.
Document: agent name (daily vs weekly), last dose date/time, presence/absence of GI symptoms, and assigned risk tier.

7) Diabetes medication protocol (adult elective cases)

Goals: avoid hypoglycemia while NPO and reduce severe hyperglycemia/ketosis. This section provides anesthesia-facing defaults; coordinate with Endocrinology for insulin pumps, brittle diabetes, pregnancy, or prolonged fasting. [5]

Oral / non-insulin injectables

  • SGLT2 inhibitors: stop ≥3 days preop (4 days for ertugliflozin). [3,4]
  • Sulfonylureas: hold DOS.
  • Metformin: hold DOS; earlier if renal dysfunction/major surgery/contrast. [5]
  • DPP-4 inhibitors: commonly hold DOS or continue; institutional preference.
  • GLP‑1 RAs: see Section 6 (risk-based; usually continue). [2]

Insulin (typical defaults; individualize)

  • Basal insulin (glargine/detemir/degludec): give 50–80% of usual dose the evening before or morning of surgery depending on case timing and glycemic control. [5]
  • NPH: often ~50% of usual morning dose on DOS. [5]
  • Rapid/short-acting (meal bolus): hold while NPO; use correctional insulin per protocol. [5]
  • Pump/CGM: coordinate a pump plan; document basal setting and intraop monitoring frequency. [5]

Target periop glucose ranges should follow institutional policy (often 80–180 mg/dL in many protocols).

If on SGLT2 inhibitor within the hold window: screen for symptoms of ketoacidosis (nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, malaise) and consider ketone testing. Treat as higher-risk even if glucose is normal (euglycemic DKA). [3,4]

8) Antithrombotics (anticoagulants / antiplatelets)

Do not stop antiplatelet therapy in patients with recent coronary stents or recent ACS without cardiology input. Document indication and date of stent/ACS, and planned procedure bleeding risk. [6]

Stop timing varies by medication, renal function, bleeding risk, and whether neuraxial/regional anesthesia is planned. Use institutional pathways aligned with CHEST perioperative antithrombotic guidance and ASRA neuraxial guidance. [9,10]

Operational rules (default)

  1. Classify procedure bleeding risk: low vs intermediate vs high/closed-space.
  2. Determine thrombotic risk: indication (AF/VTE/mechanical valve), recent VTE, recent stent/ACS.
  3. For neuraxial blocks/epidurals: follow ASRA stop/restart guidance.
  4. Record last dose and planned restart time in the anesthesia record.

Typical elective stop windows (illustrative only)

  • Warfarin: often stop ~5 days preop; verify INR prior to procedure. [9]
  • DOACs: often stop 24–72+ hours preop depending on bleeding risk and renal function. [9,10]
  • Clopidogrel: often stop ~5 days before high bleeding-risk procedures unless compelling cardiac indication. [6,10]
  • Prasugrel: often 7 days; ticagrelor often 3–5 days (procedure dependent). [10]
  • Aspirin: often continue for secondary prevention; stop for selected closed-space cases per surgeon/anesthesia consensus. [6]

These are not substitutes for formal pathways. Neuraxial timing differs and may be longer.

9) Neuraxial / regional anesthesia considerations

For neuraxial techniques (spinal/epidural) and deep plexus blocks, adhere to ASRA guidance on anticoagulant/antiplatelet timing. Regional anesthesia planning may change preop stop intervals and postoperative restart timing. [10]

Departmental recommendation: if neuraxial is planned or possible, the preop clinic note should explicitly document: (1) antithrombotic type and last dose, (2) ASRA-compliant hold interval, (3) catheter management plan (if applicable). [10]

10) Day-of-surgery checklist (anesthesia)

  1. Confirm NPO status (type and time of last intake). [1]
  2. GLP‑1 RA assessment: agent, last dose, GI symptoms, risk tier; verify whether 24-hr liquid diet was used if high risk. [2]
  3. Diabetes safety: confirm SGLT2 inhibitor hold interval; check glucose; consider ketones if symptoms/concerns. [3,4,5]
  4. Antithrombotics: document indication + last dose; neuraxial plan; confirm stop interval adherence. [9,10]
  5. Withdrawal-risk meds: beta blockers, clonidine, anticonvulsants, chronic steroids administered as planned. [6,7]
  6. Resume plan: establish postoperative restart timing for held medications, especially antithrombotics and diabetes meds.

Appendix: quick reference lists

“Continue” quick list

  • Beta blockers, CCBs, statins, clonidine
  • Inhalers/asthma meds
  • Anticonvulsants; Parkinson meds (time-sensitive)
  • Levothyroxine
  • Chronic steroids (evaluate stress-dose)
  • Chronic opioids; acetaminophen
  • Transplant immunosuppression (coordinate)

“Hold/adjust” quick list

  • SGLT2 inhibitors: hold ≥3 days (4 days for ertugliflozin)
  • Sulfonylureas: hold DOS
  • Metformin: hold DOS (earlier if renal/major surgery/contrast)
  • ACEi/ARB: hold DOS (typical)
  • Diuretics: hold DOS (typical)
  • Insulin: adjust basal; hold meal bolus while NPO
  • Antithrombotics: follow pathway; document indication + last dose
  • Herbal supplements: stop 1–2 weeks when feasible
